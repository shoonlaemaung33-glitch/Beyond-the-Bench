Why Environmental Awareness Matters

We believe in the transformative power of connecting with nature. Research shows that spending time in natural environments reduces stress, improves mental health, and increases physical wellbeing. Our parks and green spaces are essential for both environmental health and human wellness.

Beyond individual benefits, environmental awareness fosters community engagement and ecological stewardship. When we understand our local ecosystems, we become better equipped to protect them for future generations.

Local Plants & Trees

Neem (Tama)

Gives wide shade; good for community areas

Habitat: Tropical to subtropical

Uses: Traditional medicine (Ayurveda, Unani) for skin, dental, fever, and inflammation issues

Lotus & Water Lily

Distinct aquatic plants

Edibility: Lotus roots and seeds are widely eaten in Asia

Water Lily: Possess unique daily opening/closing habits linked to weather memory

Rain Tree (Yin Mar)

Provides ample shade with wide canopy, leaves close during rain to reduce water absorption.

Canopy: Up to 30m wide

Flowers: Pink and white clusters

Padauk

Myanmar's beloved national flower

Scent: A distinct sweet, woody fragranc

Appearance: Tiny, fragrant, golden-yellow blossoms that cover the tree

Local Birds

Burmese Bushlark

Migratory bird with distinctive call, known for brood parasitism (laying eggs in other birds' nests).

Diet: Primarily insects and seeds, gleaned from the ground

Habitet: Open areas with scattered bushes and trees, including sandy fields, grasslands, and cultivated land

Tree Sparrow

Found across Eurasia in farmland, edges of woods, and villages

Appearance: A small, brown bird with a distinctive chestnut-brown cap, white cheeks with a black spot, and buff undersides

Behavior: Nests in tree holes/boxes, less tied to humans than House Sparrows, often in flocks

Common Myna

A bold, adaptable Asian bird known for its brown body, black head, yellow bill, and white wing patches visible in flight

Movement: Moves on the ground instead of hopping

Diet: Omnivorous

Asian Koel

Males sing a loud "ko-o-o" to attract mates and signal nesting time for hosts; females have a "kik-kik-kik"

Appearance: Males are shiny bluish-black with red eyes; females are brown with white/buff spots and streaks

Behavior: Shy but heard more than seen, often hiding in canopies

Local Insects

Butterfly Species

A widespread Asian swallowtail known for female mimicry, where females adopt patterns of distasteful butterflies to avoid predators

Appearance: Males are black with white spots; females have varied patterns

Significance: A key pollinator, contributing to local ecosystems

Dragon Flies

Ancient, highly skilled aerial predators with huge eyes for 360Â° vision, allowing them to catch 90%+ of their insect prey

Ablities: Super hunters, can fly in any direction, hover, and change direction instantly

Color: They come in vibrant metallic colors and vary greatly in size

Fire Ants

Aggressive, invasive insects known for painful, venomous stings that form pustules

Structure: Colonies have a queen (or multiple), fertile males, and sterile female workers/soldiers

Lifespan: Queens lives for years while workers live 4-6 weeks

Mosquitos

Tiny, flying insects known as the world's deadliest animals due to disease transmission

Diet: Blood meals, Only female mosquitoes bite humans and animals for the protein needed to develop eggs; males feed on nectar

Lifespan: Adult mosquitoes live for about 2 to 4 weeks, females often live longer than males

Conservation Efforts

Plant more Natives trees

Use local species like Rain Tree, Padauk, Neem, and Bamboo

Why?: Native plants support local insects and birds better than imported species

Goal: Ecosystem preservation

Invasive Species Management

Controlling non-native species that threaten local ecosystems through monitoring and removal programs.

Approach: Monitoring programs

Tools: Biological controls

Pollution Control

Reducing air, water, and soil contamination through sustainable practices and community education.

Focus: Clean technologies

Community: Education programs

Climate Action

Addressing climate change impacts through carbon reduction, habitat restoration, and adaptation strategies.

Strategy: Carbon reduction

Adaptation: Resilience planning
